Treatment: Admit the patient for supportive management of acute pancreatitis. This includes:
1. **Fluid resuscitation:** Aggressive intravenous hydration with isotonic crystalloids (e.g., Lactated Ringer's solution) to correct hypovolemia and maintain electrolyte balance. Monitor urine output and electrolytes closely. Address the low potassium with supplementation as needed.
2. **Pain management:** Administer intravenous analgesics (e.g., hydromorphone or fentanyl) for pain control. Avoid morphine due to the potential for sphincter of Oddi spasm.
3. **Nutritional support:** Initiate nothing by mouth (NPO) status initially.  If the patient shows improvement and pain subsides, start with clear liquids and advance the diet as tolerated.  If the patient continues to have pain or develops complications, consider enteral nutrition via nasojejunal feeding tube.  For severe cases, total parenteral nutrition (TPN) may be necessary.
4. **Monitoring:** Closely monitor vital signs, including heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate, and oxygen saturation.  Monitor for signs of complications such as infection, pancreatic necrosis, or pseudocyst formation.
5. **Alcohol cessation counseling:** Reinforce the importance of abstaining from alcohol to prevent future episodes of pancreatitis.  Provide resources for ongoing support and treatment for alcohol use disorder.
6. **Further investigations:** Once the acute phase has subsided, consider imaging studies such as an abdominal ultrasound or CT scan to assess for gallstones, pancreatic pseudocysts, or other complications.  If the etiology of pancreatitis remains unclear, consider further evaluation for other causes, such as hypertriglyceridemia or hypercalcemia.
7. **Follow-up:** Arrange for close follow-up with a gastroenterologist after discharge to monitor for recurrence and manage any long-term complications.
