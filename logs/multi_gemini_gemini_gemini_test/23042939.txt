Treatment:
1. **NPO:** Initially, the patient should be kept NPO to allow the pancreas to rest.
2. **IV Fluids:** Aggressive IV fluid resuscitation is crucial to maintain hydration and electrolyte balance.
3. **Pain Management:** Administer analgesics (e.g., IV morphine or hydromorphone) for pain control.
4. **Antibiotics:** While not routinely indicated in mild gallstone pancreatitis, antibiotics (e.g., Piperacillin/Tazobactam) should be considered given the elevated bilirubin and potential for cholangitis.
5. **ERCP:**  Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP) is indicated to relieve biliary obstruction and remove any stones in the common bile duct. This should be performed within 24-48 hours of presentation.
6. **Cholecystectomy:**  Laparoscopic cholecystectomy is the definitive treatment for gallstone pancreatitis and should be performed during the same hospital admission or shortly after, once the pancreatitis has resolved.
7. **Monitoring:** Closely monitor vital signs, lab values (lipase, amylase, liver function tests, and complete blood count), and clinical status for signs of complications such as pancreatic necrosis, pseudocyst formation, or infection.
8. **Nutritional Support:** If the patient remains NPO for an extended period, consider enteral nutrition via a nasogastric or nasojejunal feeding tube.  If enteral feeding is not tolerated, parenteral nutrition may be necessary.
9. **Follow-up:** After discharge, the patient should follow up with a gastroenterologist or surgeon for ongoing management and monitoring.  Address the incidental finding of mild right hydronephrosis with a repeat ultrasound in 4-6 weeks.
