Treatment:  Given the suspicion for porphyria, the following steps are recommended:

1. **Confirm the diagnosis:**  Order a 24-hour urine collection for porphyrin precursors (delta-aminolevulinic acid and porphobilinogen).  A blood test for porphyrins can also be helpful. Genetic testing can confirm the specific type of porphyria.
2. **Manage acute attacks:**  Treatment focuses on symptom relief and preventing complications.
    * **Pain management:**  Intravenous analgesics, such as morphine or hydromorphone, may be necessary. Avoid NSAIDs, as they can exacerbate porphyria.
    * **Nausea and vomiting:** Antiemetics can be used to control these symptoms.
    * **Intravenous fluids:**  Adequate hydration is essential.
    * **Carbohydrate loading:** A high-carbohydrate diet or intravenous glucose can help suppress the overproduction of porphyrins.
    * **Hemin:**  Hemin is a specific treatment for acute porphyria attacks and can shorten their duration.
3. **Long-term management:**
    * **Avoid triggers:**  Identify and avoid factors that can precipitate attacks, such as certain medications (e.g., barbiturates, sulfonamides), alcohol, smoking, fasting, and stress.
    * **Genetic counseling:**  If a specific type of porphyria is diagnosed, genetic counseling can be offered to the patient and her family.
4. **Address anemia:**  Investigate the cause of the patient's anemia. Iron supplementation may be necessary if iron deficiency is present.  Monitor hemoglobin and hematocrit levels.
5. **Monitor for pre-eclampsia:**  Given the patient's history of prior SABs and current presentation, continue to monitor her blood pressure and urine protein for signs of pre-eclampsia.
